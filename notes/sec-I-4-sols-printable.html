    Worksheet \(\S I.4\text{:}\) Connections to (classical) Logic
    

    1. Motivation: Logic.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Determining whether something is true or false (or telling a computer how to do so) is fundamental in computing. This requires logic. We’ll introduce some basic concepts and notation, as well as connections to sets.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Propositions and Operations.

    🔗
    Solution.
    In classical logic, we deal with statements called propositions: statements that are unambiguously true or false.
    🔗
    "My name is Jeff" is a proposition because it is unambiguously true. "It is too hot today" is not proposition. "\(6-4=0\)" is a proposition because it is unambiguously false.
    🔗
    There are a few basic operations we can perform on the propositions \(p\) and \(q\text{:}\)
    1. \(\neg p\text{:}\) "not \(p\)", called the negation of \(p\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(p \wedge q\text{:}\) "\(p\) and \(q\)", called the conjunction of \(p\) and \(q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(p \vee q\text{:}\) "\(p\) or \(q\)", called the disjunction of \(p\) and \(q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Truth Tables.

    Operations on propositions result in new propositions. To keep track of the resulting truth values, we can use truth tables.
    🔗
    \(p\) \(\neg p\)
    T
    F
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \wedge q\)
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \vee q\)
    Solution.
    \(p\) \(\neg p\)
    T F
    F T
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \wedge q\)
    T T T
    F F F
    T F F
    F T F
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \vee q\)
    T T T
    F F F
    T F T
    F T T
    🔗
    🔗

    4. Logic and Sets.

    Solution.
    Propositions are connected to sets because given a predicate \(p(x)\) (a proposition with a variable), we can build a corresponding set \(P\) such that \(p(x)\) is true exactly when \(x \in P\text{.}\) Under this correspondence,
    1. \(\neg p\) corresponds to \(P^C\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(p \wedge q\) corresponds to \(P \cap Q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(p \vee q\) corresponds to \(P \cup Q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Another important consequence is that we inherit all of the properties of sets (like distributive laws and DeMorgan’s Laws).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Conditional Statements.

    We can combine propositions in two more important ways:
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(p \to q\text{:}\) "If \(p\) then \(q\)", called a conditional statement
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(p \longleftrightarrow q\text{:}\) "If \(p\) if and only if \(q\)", called a biconditional statement
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The two propositions above have the following truth table:
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \to q\) \(q \to p\) \(p \longleftrightarrow q\)
    T T T T T
    F F T T T
    T F F T F
    F T T F F
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Example: Determining truth values of complicated propositions.

    Determine the truth table for the proposition
    \begin{equation*} p \to q \to \neg p \vee q\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \to q\) \(\neg p\) \(\neg p \vee q\) \((p \to q) \to (\neg p \vee q)\)
    T T T F T T
    F F T T T T
    T F F F F T
    F T T T T T
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    7. Important Conditional Statements.

    For the conditional statement \(p \to q\text{,}\) we can form three other (related) conditional statements:
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\neg p \to \neg q\text{,}\) called the inverse statement
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(q \to p\text{,}\) called the converse statement
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\neg q \to \neg p\text{,}\) called the contrapositive statement
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    8. Practice.

    (a)

    Let \(p\) be the proposition "It is snowing" and \(q\) be the proposition "CSU is closed for the day". Translate the following to English sentences.
    1. \(\displaystyle q \to p\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \neg p \to \neg q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle p \wedge q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle p \longleftrightarrow q\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. If CSU is closed for the day, then it is snowing.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If it is not snowing, then CSU is not closed for the day.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. It is snowing and CSU is closed for the day.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. It is snowing if and only if CSU is closed for the day. (Equivalently, If it is snowing then CSU is closed for the day, and if CSU is closed for the day then it is snowing.)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Use truth tables to show that \(p \to q\) is logically equivalent (i.e., has the same truth table) to \(\neg q \to \neg p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(p\) \(q\) \(p \to q\) \(\neg p\) \(\neg q\) \(\neg q \to \neg p\)
    T T T F F T
    F F T T T T
    T F F F T F
    F T T T F T
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Without using truth tables, explain why the inverse and converse of \(p \to q\) are logically equivalent.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The inverse statement is the contrapositive of the converse statement. By the result of the previous question, these are logically equivalent.
    🔗
