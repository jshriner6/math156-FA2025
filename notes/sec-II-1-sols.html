    1. Motivation: Relations.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Now that we know a lot about sets and set notation, we can talk about how sets can interact in meaningful ways. A fundamental interaction is called a relation.
    🔗

    🔗

    2. Definition and Example: Relation.

    A relation between sets \(A\) and \(B\) is
    🔗
    Solution.
    is a subset \(R \subset A \times B\text{.}\) We often indicate elements \((a,b) \in R\) by \(a \sim_R b\) or \(aRb\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Consider the sets \(\N\) and \(P\) of prime numbers. We’ll define a relation \(R\) on \(\N \times P\) by saying \(aRb\) if \(b\) divides \(a\text{.}\) Then we can note that
    1. \(\displaystyle 4R2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(4 \not R 3\) (\(3\) does not divide \(4\))
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(2 \not R 4 \) (\(4\) does not divide \(2\))
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(12 \not R 6\) (\((12,6) \not \in \N \times P\))
      🔗
      🔗

    3. Familiar Relations.

    The following are all relations on \(\R \times \R\text{.}\) Write at least three elements in each relation as elements of \(\R \times \R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\lt\text{.}\) Examples are \((1,2), (\pi,4), (\sqrt{2}, 2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\leq\text{.}\) Examples are \((3,3), (0,2), (-4, -1)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(=\text{.}\) Examples are \((3,3), (1.8,1.8), (-1,-1) \text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗

    4. Representations of Relations.

    We can represent relations in multiple ways. Let \(A= \{1, 2, 3, 4\}\text{,}\) and \(R \subset A \times A\) be the relation defined by "\(\lt\)".
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can represent \(A\) as
    1. a subset of \(A \times A\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} R= \{(1,2), (1,3), (1,4), (2,3), (2,4), (3,4) \} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. a table. We can represent \((a,b) \in A \times A\) as
      \(a\) \(b\)
      \(1\) \(2\)
      \(1\) \(3\)
      \(1\) \(4\)
      \(2\) \(3\)
      \(2\) \(4\)
      \(3\) \(4\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. directed graphs. You can see directed graphs being used in a similar example in Figure 1 of section II.1.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    If our relation is on \(\R \times \R\text{,}\) there is another familiar representation as a graph in the coordinate plane.
    🔗
    For example, the relation \(R_1 = \{(x,y) \in \R \times \R | (x,y) =( x, x^2)\}\) is just the equation \(y=x^2\text{.}\) We can view this relation as the graph of \(y=x^2\).
    🔗
    A similar example is the relation \(R_2 = \{(x,y) \in \R \times \R | x^2 + y^2 = 1\}\text{.}\) This relation is viewed as the graph of a circle of radius one.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Definitions: Source, target, domain, and range.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(R \subset A \times B\) be a relation.
    🔗
    The set \(A\) is called the source of \(R\text{,}\) and the set \(B\) is called the target of \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The set
    \begin{equation*} D = \{a \in A | \exists b \in B, (a,b) \in R\} \end{equation*}
    is the domain of \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The set
    \begin{equation*} G = \{ b \in B | \exists a \in A, (a,b) \in R\} \end{equation*}
    is the range of \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Practice: Domain and Range.

    Let \(S=\{1,2,3,4,5\}\text{,}\) and \(R \subset S \times S\) be the relation \(\{(a,b) \in S \times S | b \lt a\}\text{.}\) What is the domain? What is the range?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The domain of \(R\) is \(\{2,3,4,5\}\text{,}\) while the range of \(R\) is \(\{1,2,3,4\}\text{.}\)
