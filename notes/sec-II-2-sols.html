    Worksheet \(\S II.2\text{:}\) Complements, Converse, and Composition
    

    1. Motivation: Relations from Relations.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Given a relation \(R\text{,}\) there can be other meaningful relations that are implied. There are some natural operations on \(R\) that allow us to express these implied relations explicitly.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. First Examples.

    Let \(S= \{\text{all people}\}\text{.}\) We can define a relation \(R\) on \(S_1 \times S_2\) (these are both copies of the set \(S\text{,}\) we are just using subscripts to distinguish copies for convenience later) by the rule "is a parent of". Let’s explore some implied relations in this example and how we can construct them explicitly.
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. "Is not a parent of" is the complement of \(R\text{,}\) written \(R^C\text{.}\) It is defined as the set
      \begin{equation*} R^C = \{(a,b) \in S_1 \times S_2 | (a,b) \not \in R\} \subset S_1 \times S_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. "Is a child of" is the converse of \(R\text{,}\) which we will write as \(R^T\text{.}\) It is defined as the set
      \begin{equation*} R^T = \{(b,a) \in S_2 \times S_1 | (a,b) \in R\} \subset S_2 \times S_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. "Is a grandparent of" is the composition of two copies of the relation \(R\text{,}\) which we will write as \(R_2 \subset S_3 \times S_1\) and \(R_1 \subset S_1 \times S_2\text{.}\) It is defined as the set
      \begin{equation*} R_1 \circ R_2 = \{(a,c) \in S_3 \times S_2 | \exists b, (a,b) \in R_2 \text{ and } (b,c) \in R_1\} \text{.} \end{equation*}
      Note \(R_1 \circ R_2 \subset S_3 \times S_2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Practice: Complement and Converse.

    Let \(A=\{1,2,3,4,5\}\) and \(B=\{1, 4, 5\}\text{.}\) Let \(R\) be the relation on \(A \times B\) defined by "\(\lt\)".
    🔗

    (a)

    Without building \(R^C\text{,}\) describe what you think \(R^C\) is.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Write out all elements of \(R^C\) to either confirm or refine your previous answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} R= \{(1,4), (1,5), (2,4), (2,5), (3,4), (3,5), (4,5)\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} R^C = \{(1,1), (2,1), (3,1), (4,1), (4,4), (5,1), (5,4), (5,5)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the relation "\(\geq\)".
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Without building \(R^T\text{,}\) describe what you think \(R^T\) is.
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Write out all elements of \(R^T\) to either confirm or refine your previous answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the relation \(R\) written out above, we can see that
    \begin{equation*} R^T = \{(4,1), (5,1), (4,2), (5,2), (4,3), (5,3), (5,4)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the "\(\gt\)" relation on \(B \times A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    True or False: \((R^C)^T = (R^T)^C\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    This is true. Take \(R^T\) computed above and write down the complement in \(B \times A\text{,}\) then verify this is the same as the set obtained by swapping all the coordinates in \(R^C\) computed above.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    🔗

    (a)

    (b)

    Let \(S\) be the relation "\(\gt\)" on \(B \times C\text{.}\) Represent \(S\) as a digraph.
    🔗
    Solution.
    See notes from class, or a similar example in Figure 4 of section II.2.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Only one of the relations \(R \circ S\) or \(S \circ R\) makes sense. Explain why one does not, and then write the elements of the other.
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(R \subset A \times B\) and \(S \subset B \times C\text{.}\) Therefore, \(R \circ S\) does not make sense because we’d be trying to take elements of \(B \times C\) and "connect" them with elements in \(A \times B\text{,}\) but \(C\) and \(A\) are different sets.
    🔗
    \(S \circ R \) does make sense and its elements are
    \begin{equation*} S \circ R = \{(1,-1), (1,2), (1,3), (2,-1), (2,2), (2,3), (3,-1), (3,2), (3,3)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
