    4. Observations: Modular Arithmetic.

    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Every element has an additive inverse. For example, \(2+1 \equiv 0 (\mod 3)\) and \(2+2 \equiv 0 (\mod 4)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. In \(\Z / 3\Z\text{,}\) every non-zero element also has a multiplicative inverse. For example, \(2 \cdot 2 \equiv 1 (\mod 3)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. In \(\Z / 4\Z\text{,}\) multiplication is strange. For example, \(2x \equiv 1 (\mod 4)\) has no solution, and \(2 \cdot 2 \equiv 0 (\mod 4)\text{,}\) even though \(2\) is non-zero!
      🔗
      A helpful fact is that \(\overline{a}\) has a multiplicative inverse in \(\Z / m\Z\) if and only if the greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(m\) is \(1\text{.}\) This makes \(\Z / p\Z\text{,}\) for \(p\) prime, special!
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Example: Solving an Equation.

    Solve \(\overline{5}x + \overline{3} = \overline{1}\) in \(\Z / 8\Z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\begin{align} \overline{5}x + \overline{3} \amp= \overline{1} \\ \overline{5}x \amp= \overline{-2} = \overline{6} \\ x \amp= \overline{6} \cdot \overline{5} = \overline{30} =\overline{6} \end{align}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Practice: Solving an Equation.

    Solve \(\overline{4}x + \overline{3} = \overline{3}\) in \(\Z / 8\Z\text{,}\) then in \(\Z / 7\Z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    In \(\Z / 8\Z\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \(\begin{align} \overline{4}x + \overline{3} \amp= \overline{3} \\ \overline{4}x \amp= \overline{0} \\ \end{align}\)
    🔗
    \(\overline{4}\) has no multiplicative inverse in \(\Z / 8\Z\text{,}\) which means there are either no solutions or multiple solutions. In this case we can check all elements to see that \(x=\overline{0}, \overline{2}, \overline{4}, \overline{6}\) are all solutions.
    🔗
    In \(\Z / 7\Z\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \(\begin{align} \overline{4}x + \overline{3} \amp= \overline{3} \\ \overline{4}x \amp= \overline{0} \\ x \amp= \overline{0} \cdot \overline{2} = \overline{0} \end{align}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
