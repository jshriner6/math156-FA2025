    Worksheet \(\S VI.3\text{:}\) Optimization
    

    1. Motivation: Optimization.

    🔗
    Solution.
    One important application of derivatives is to optimize functions, such as answering questions like "What dimensions will maximize area?" and "How much product will maximize profit?". We’ll look at how to answer such questions in this section.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Optimization: Closed Intervals.

    Fact (Extreme Value Theorem): Let \(f: [a,b] \to \R\) be continuous. Then
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(f\) has both an absolute maximum and absolute minimum value on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is a methodical process for finding the absolute extrema in this case:
    1. Find all critical numbers of \(f\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Compute \(f(a), f(b),\text{,}\) and \(f(c)\) for all critical numbers \(c\) found in the previous part.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Pick the largest (this is the absolute maximum) and smallest (this is the absolute minimum) output values.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Example: Closed Intervals.

    Find the absolute maximum and minimum of \(f: [1,4] \to \R\) where \(f(x)=2x^3-9x^2+10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We compute \(f'(x) = 6x^2 - 18x\) to find the critical numbers \(x=0,3\) of \(f\text{.}\) However, only \(x=3\) is in the closed interval \([1,4]\text{,}\) so we ignore \(x=0\text{.}\) Then we compute \(f(1)=3, f(4)=-6\text{,}\) and \(f(3)=-17\) to conclude that \(f\) has an absolute maximum value of \(y=3\) at \(x=1\) and an absolute minimum value of \(y=-17\) at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4. Example: Application.

    A farmer has \(60\) feet of fencing to enclose a rectangular field, which borders a canal (so needs no fencing on that side). What is the largest area they can enclose?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The relevant function to optimize is \(A=xy\text{,}\) where \(2y+x =60\text{,}\) or \(x=60-2y\text{.}\) This means the area function becomes \(A(y) = y(60-2y) = 60y-2y^2\text{,}\) and the relevant interval for \(y\) is \([0,30]\text{.}\) Then \(A'(y)= 60-4y\text{,}\) so \(y=15\) is a critical number of \(A\) in the interval \([0,30]\text{.}\) Testing end points and the crtical number, we see that \(A\) has an absolute maximum of \(450\) square feet, occurring when \(x=30\) feet and \(y=15\) feet.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Optimization: Open Intervals.

    Fact: If \(f\) is continuous and has a single critical point \(c \in (a,b)\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    Solution.
    if \(f(c)\) is a local minimum it is also an absolute minimum. Similarly, if \(f(c)\) is a local maximum it is also an absolute maximum.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Example: Open Intervals.

    Let \(f(x) = 2 + \frac{3}{1+(x+1)^2}\text{.}\) Find the absolute maximum of \(f\) on \(\R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We compute \(f'(x) = \frac{-6(x+1)}{(1+(x+1)^2)^2}\text{,}\) and see that \(x=-1 \) is the only critical number of \(f\) on \(\R\text{.}\) Using a first derivative sign chart, we can classify \(x=-1\) as a local maximum, and therefore \(f(-1)=5\) is also an absolute maximum of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    7. Practice: Optimization.

    Find the absolute maximum and minimum of \(g: [-6,0] \to \R\text{,}\) where \(g(x) =e^x \cdot x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(g'(x) = e^x x (x+2)\text{,}\) so \(x=0,-2\) are critical numbers of \(g\text{.}\) Testing \(g(0), g(-6)\text{,}\) and \(g(-2)\text{,}\) we can conclude that \(g\) has an absolute maximum at \(x=-2\) and an absolute minimum at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    8. Practice: Application.

    You need to design a box with a square base and open top whose volume is 864 cubic inches. What is the minimum surface area of such a box?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The surface area equation is \(S=x^2 + 4xy\) with the condition that \(x^2y=864\text{.}\) This means we can write \(S(x) = x^2 +3456x^{-1}\text{,}\) with the relevant interval for \(x\) being \((0,\infty)\text{.}\) We can compute \(S'(x) = 2x - 3456x^{-2}\text{,}\) so critical numbers of \(S\) are \(x=0, 12\text{,}\) but we can ignore \(x=0\text{.}\) We can test \(x=12\) using the first derivative test to conclude that it is a local minimum. Since it is the only critical number in \((0,\infty)\text{,}\) it is also the location of an absolute minimum. Therefore, the minimum surface area is \(S(12) = 432\) square inches.
    🔗
